## The Mechanics of the "Just Pay Shipping" Scam

[cite_start]The psychology behind the free trial trap is incredibly effective[cite: 371]. [cite_start]Marketers know consumers are hesitant to drop $70 on an unverified supplement[cite: 372]. [cite_start]To lower the barrier to entry, they offer a seemingly risk-free proposition: "Try a 30-day supply on us, just pay $4.95 for shipping and handling"[cite: 373].

[cite_start]When you agree to pay that shipping fee, you are almost always required to check a small box agreeing to the company's Terms and Conditions[cite: 375]. [cite_start]What the main sales page conveniently omits is that by accepting the trial, you are actively enrolling in a negative option billing program (also known as a continuity program)[cite: 377]. 

[cite_start]Precisely 14 to 30 days after your initial order, a massive charge—often upwards of $80—will silently hit your credit card for the next month's supply[cite: 379]. 

## The Sight Fresh Case Study

[cite_start]To understand how this operates in the wild, we can look at the billing models of heavily marketed products like Sight Fresh[cite: 380]. [cite_start]These brands frequently utilize high-pressure sales tactics, countdown timers, and "limited supply" warnings to rush consumers through the checkout process[cite: 381]. 

[cite_start]The primary goal is to get you to input your payment details before you have time to thoroughly read the subscription terms[cite: 382]. [cite_start]By burying auto-ship subscription traps deep in the fine print, these companies ensure a steady stream of recurring revenue[cite: 383]. [cite_start]Because the charges correspond with an automated monthly delivery, it can take months for the average consumer to notice the drain on a crowded bank statement[cite: 384]. 

## The Nightmare of Canceling

[cite_start]Spotting the charge is only half the battle[cite: 386]. [cite_start]Companies that rely on this deceptive billing model deliberately make their cancellation processes incredibly convoluted[cite: 387]. If you fall victim to this, you will typically encounter:

* [cite_start]**The Customer Service Loop:** The phone number provided on your bank statement leads to an endless automated menu or a full voicemail box[cite: 388].
* [cite_start]**The Email Black Hole:** Messages sent to their support addresses often bounce back or receive automated replies that do not actually process your cancellation[cite: 389].
* [cite_start]**The Nuclear Option:** In many cases, victims are forced to call their bank, report the credit card as lost or stolen, and wait for a brand new card to be issued just to stop the continuous billing[cite: 390].
